# Dissertation Outline

**Title:** *Captured by Design: Central Bank Independence as Structural Regulatory Capture*

**Research Question:** Why do private financial interests consistently defend the Federal Reserve's independence against legislative revision?

**Core thesis:** Financial interests are not merely *captured* by the Fed — they are *ingrained* in it from the start, through governance architecture, personnel socialisation, and the epistemic framework used to evaluate independence itself.

---

## 1. Introduction (~750w)

- JPMorgan/Powell paradox as hook
- The asymmetry: financial sector challenges the Fed when it imposes constraints (ABA/BPI stress test litigation) but defends it against democratic oversight — this asymmetry is the evidence
- RQ + preview of argument
- Epistemic capture as central contribution
- Note: "ingrained" rather than "captured" — the Fed was never fully public in the first place (Downey)

---

## 2. Background and Theory (~1,600w)

### 2.1 Regulatory Capture (~600w)
- Dal Bó (2006) typology: broad definition — monetary policy explicitly included
- Stigler-Peltzman black box: industry acquires regulation for its own benefit; mechanism unexplained
- Three-tier hierarchy: political principal (Congress) → regulator (Fed) → regulated firm (banks)
- Asymmetric information as origin of regulatory discretion
- Revolving door mechanism
- Kwak (2013): cultural capture — group identification, deference to high-status executives, face-to-face socialisation; no explicit coordination required
- Instrumental vs. structural distinction: instrumental requires observable coordination; structural operates through institutional design
- Epistemic capture as extension: structural capture operates through the production of legitimate knowledge

### 2.2 The Economics Literature on CBI (~600w)

#### Theoretical foundations
- Kydland and Prescott (1977): time-inconsistency — governments cannot credibly commit; discretion produces suboptimal outcomes
- Barro and Gordon (1983): inflationary bias under discretion; reputation as only partial substitute; rules as optimal
- Rogoff (1985): conservative central banker — explicitly third-best; acknowledges employment variance cost; infinite inflation aversion ruled out as suboptimal; first-best option itself encodes contestable neoclassical assumptions about labour markets

#### Empirical development
- Grilli, Masciandaro, Tabellini (1991): first systematic cross-country CBI index
- Cukierman, Webb, and Neyapti (1992): legal vs. behavioural independence; foundational dataset
- Alesina and Summers (1993): CBI correlates with lower inflation, no output cost — the free lunch claim; authors' own caveat ("not conclusive") dropped from policy consensus
- Romelli (2022): CBIE index — 154 countries, 1972–2017; objectives dimension doubles; presented approvingly
- Adrian, Khan, Menand (2024): new IMF index; survey-weighted; acknowledges democratic oversight tension

#### Shared features across all five
- De jure measurement: observable legal characteristics, not behaviour
- Inherited theoretical prior: time-inconsistency framework assumed, not tested
- Price stability as primary benchmark; employment mandate at most a control variable
- Free lunch claim quietly drops Rogoff's acknowledged trade-off

### 2.3 The Political Science Literature on CBI (~400w)
- Posen (1995): financial sector sources of CBI — independence requires active political coalition; financial sector provides it
- Adolph (2018): shadow principals — career incentives shape preferences pre- and post-office; missing critical junctures post-2008
- Kwak (2013): cultural capture at the Fed — securities business expansion, consumer protection non-enforcement, capital requirement relaxation all documented
- Zingales (2013): economists' capture — career incentives, funding dependencies, professional socialisation produce pro-market bias
- GAO reports (2017, 2019): official government acknowledgment of capture risk at the Fed and FDIC
- Federal Advisory Council finding: only the four largest banks on the FAC demonstrably influence the Fed on monetary policy (Federal Funds rate)
- Two non-interacting epistemic communities: economics and political science study CBI without meaningful cross-citation
- Economics community institutionally embedded; political science critique has no institutional footprint
- Flag: non-interaction is itself evidence — developed in 4.1

## 3. Methodology (~950w)

### 3.1 Research design (~300w)
- Theory-building single case study: the Federal Reserve
- Process tracing (Beach and Pedersen 2013; Bennett and Checkel 2015)
- Limitation: epistemic capture hard to observe directly; triangulation across three independent streams
- Framing note: "ingrained" rather than "captured" — argument is about original design, not subsequent takeover

### 3.2 Causal chain and evidentiary tests (~400w)
- Step 1 — hoop test: epistemological assumptions in pro-CBI literature encode financial sector preferences
- Step 2 — hoop test: assumptions transmitted through institutional vessels (personnel, speeches)
- Step 3 — smoking gun: legislative revision pre-emptively foreclosed; defences of inaction rooted in same assumptions
- Van Evera (1997) evidentiary framework

### 3.3 Rival hypothesis (~250w)
- Posen (PIIE 2025): technocratic legitimacy — CBI works; no revision because no reason to revise
- Insufficient: explains outcome but not assumptions, personnel patterns, or foreclosed reform agenda
- Rival hypothesis is itself a product of the epistemic environment under scrutiny — this is the circularity

## 4. Analysis (~5,750w)

### 4.1 Epistemological Assumptions in the Pro-CBI Literature (~1,500w)
*Validates Zingales empirically — the literature itself is the evidence*

- Price stability as unexamined social optimum — inherited from Kydland-Prescott, never defended
- Rogoff's third-best treated as first-best; free lunch claim drops his acknowledged trade-off
- Rogoff's first-best encodes contestable neoclassical assumptions about labour markets — bias runs deeper than the conservative banker solution
- De jure measurement conflates mandate content with operational autonomy
- Powell example: congressional mandate revision toward employment would *lower* Romelli score — proves circularity
- US/Turkey anomaly: US scores below Turkey on de jure indices — yet Turkey is Posen's own paradigm case of lost independence; internal inconsistency
- Two non-interacting communities demonstrated textually: Romelli does not cite Dal Bó; PIIE contributors do not engage capture literature
- Schneider, Strahan and Yang (2023) *Review of Finance*: peer-reviewed capture framework applied to Fed stress testing — argument is established even if not yet applied to monetary policy
- Economics community institutionally embedded; political science has no footprint — precisely what Zingales predicts
- Conclusion: assumptions systematically favour creditor interests; literature cannot neutrally evaluate the institution it legitimates

### 4.2 Vessels of Transmission (~1,800w)
*Assumptions travel from academic literature into institutional discourse through specific channels*

#### 4.2.1 Governors' speeches (~900w)
- Downey: the Fed was never fully public — reserve bank boards two-thirds banker/banker-appointees; primary dealer system concentrates monetary transmission; design embeds financial interests before capture is required
- Bernanke (*The Courage to Act*, 2015): Congress did not understand QE — Dal Bó's asymmetric information mechanism confirmed by the Fed's own chair
- PIIE 2025: defends CBI while acknowledging capture risk only in supervisory functions; monetary policy mandate treated as beyond democratic contestation
- Adolph (2018): greater legal independence amplifies shadow principal influence
- Historical note — Rockefeller and Wriston: financial sector critique of the Fed has always targeted specific *constraints* (gold convertibility, Regulation Q, price controls) not independence itself; after Volcker, critique flips to attacking looseness (Druckenmiller); the asymmetry runs historically deep

#### 4.2.2 Revolving door (~900w)
- ABA revolving door: Olson (ABA president 1986–87 → Fed governor 2001–06); Duke (ABA chair 2004–05 → Fed governor 2007–13)
- Bowman: community bank background → Kansas banking commissioner → Trump nomination → Vice Chair for Supervision; SVB framed as management failure; supervision staff cut 30%; post-SVB reforms reversed
- Wirsching (2018): finance-sector backgrounds empirically predict deregulatory outcomes
- Federal Advisory Council: four largest banks demonstrably influence Federal Funds rate — formal governance channel for financial sector preferences
- Kwak (2013): cultural capture operates through group identification and socialisation, not bribes

### 4.3 Legislative Blockage (~2,450w)
*Defences of legislative inaction are rooted in the same assumptions exposed in 4.1 — circularity is the argument*

- Volcker 1979: regime shift toward price stability with no legal change; dual mandate operationally overwritten without democratic revision; Downey: dual mandate "meant little operationally" until Volcker
- Lindblom (1982): market as prison — mandate revision triggers volatility without lobbying; UK banking sector growth (~100% to ~450% of GDP since 1970s) illustrates structural deterrent growth
- Dodd-Frank as failed critical juncture: reform channelled into adjustments foreclosing fundamental revision; QE recapitalised financial sector, increasing political power when reform pressure peaked (Jacobs and King 2018 — "political multiplier effect")
- ABA/BPI stress test litigation: challenges Fed when it imposes constraints, defends it against democratic oversight — asymmetry is the evidence; pre-emptive capture of reform agenda
- McCubbins and Schwartz (1984): fire alarm oversight — financial sector ensures alarms not pulled on mandate questions
- Bernanke/asymmetric information: Congress cannot revise what it cannot understand
- PIIE 2025: defences of independence reproduce assumptions of 4.1; expert community advising Congress shares financial sector assumptions
- Rival hypothesis engagement: technocratic legitimacy explains outcome not mechanism; rival hypothesis is itself a product of captured epistemic environment; requires three independent coincidences where structural capture explains all with one mechanism

## 5. Conclusion (~650w)

- Epistemic capture as unifying mechanism: assumptions produced in captured environment → transmitted through personnel and institutional discourse → legislative revision made structurally unthinkable
- The "ingrained" framing: financial interests were never external to the Fed; they were designed in from the start
- Contribution 1: extends Dal Bó/Kwak capture typology to monetary policy institutions
- Contribution 2: CBI measurement encodes preferences of a captured epistemic community — consensus is circular; literature cannot neutrally evaluate the institution it legitimates
- Real-world implication: policy consensus built on literature shaped by the interests it purports to study
- Caveat: categorically distinct from authoritarian executive interference (Trump/Powell) — present argument is about democratic design, not executive override
- Closing: the question is not whether the Fed should be independent from political interference — it is whether financial interests are so ingrained in the institution and its legitimating literature that democratic revision has been made structurally unthinkable
