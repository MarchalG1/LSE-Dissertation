# Dissertation Outline

**Title:** *Captured by Design: Central Bank Independence as Structural Regulatory Capture*

**Research Question:** Why do private financial interests consistently defend the Federal Reserve's independence against legislative revision?

---

## 1. Introduction (~750w)

- JPMorgan/Powell paradox as hook
- RQ + preview of argument
- Epistemic capture as central contribution

---

## 2. Background and Theory (~1,600w)

### 2.1 Regulatory Capture (~600w)
- Dal Bó (2006) typology: broad definition — monetary policy explicitly included
- Stigler-Peltzman black box: industry acquires regulation for its own benefit; mechanism unexplained
- Three-tier hierarchy: political principal (Congress) → regulator (Fed) → regulated firm (banks)
- Asymmetric information as origin of regulatory discretion
- Revolving door mechanism
- Instrumental vs. structural distinction: instrumental requires observable coordination; structural operates through institutional design — no coordination required
- Epistemic capture as extension: structural capture can operate through the production of legitimate knowledge

### 2.2 The Economics Literature on CBI (~600w)

#### Theoretical foundations
- Kydland and Prescott (1977): time-inconsistency — governments cannot credibly commit; discretion produces suboptimal outcomes
- Barro and Gordon (1983): inflationary bias under discretion; reputation as only partial substitute; rules as optimal
- Rogoff (1985): conservative central banker — third-best solution; acknowledges employment variance cost; infinite inflation aversion explicitly ruled out as suboptimal

#### Empirical development
- Grilli, Masciandaro, Tabellini (1991): first systematic cross-country CBI index
- Cukierman, Webb, and Neyapti (1992): legal vs. behavioural independence; foundational dataset
- Alesina and Summers (1993): CBI correlates with lower inflation, no output cost — the free lunch claim
- Romelli (2022): CBIE index — 154 countries, 1972–2017; objectives dimension doubles; presented approvingly
- Adrian, Khan, Menand (2024): new IMF index; survey-weighted; acknowledges reporting requirements as democratic oversight

#### Shared features across all five
- De jure measurement: observable legal characteristics, not behaviour
- Inherited theoretical prior: time-inconsistency framework assumed, not tested
- Price stability as primary benchmark; employment mandate at most a control variable
- Free lunch claim: lower inflation at no output cost

### 2.3 The Political Science Literature on CBI (~400w)
- Posen (1995): financial sector sources of CBI — independence requires active political coalition; financial sector provides it
- Adolph (2018): shadow principals — career incentives shape preferences pre- and post-office; missing critical junctures post-2008
- Zingales (2013): economists' capture — career incentives, funding dependencies, professional socialisation produce pro-market bias
- Two non-interacting epistemic communities: economics and political science study CBI without meaningful cross-citation
- Economics community institutionally embedded; political science capture literature has no equivalent footprint
- Flag: this non-interaction is itself evidence — to be developed in 4.1

## 3. Methodology (~950w)

### 3.1 Research design (~300w)
- Theory-building single case study: the Federal Reserve
- Process tracing (Beach and Pedersen 2013; Bennett and Checkel 2015)
- Limitation: structural/epistemic capture hard to observe directly; triangulation across three independent streams

### 3.2 Causal chain and evidentiary tests (~400w)
- Step 1 — hoop test: epistemological assumptions in the pro-CBI literature encode financial sector preferences
- Step 2 — hoop test: those assumptions are transmitted through institutional vessels (personnel, speeches)
- Step 3 — smoking gun: legislative revision pre-emptively foreclosed; defences of inaction rooted in the same assumptions
- Van Evera (1997) evidentiary framework

### 3.3 Rival hypothesis (~250w)
- Posen (PIIE 2025): technocratic legitimacy — CBI works; no revision because no reason to revise
- Insufficient: explains outcome but not the assumptions, the personnel patterns, or the foreclosed reform agenda
- Rival hypothesis is itself a product of the epistemic environment under scrutiny

## 4. Analysis (~5,750w)

### 4.1 Epistemological Assumptions in the Pro-CBI Literature (~1,500w)
*Validates Zingales empirically — the literature itself is the evidence*

- Price stability as unexamined social optimum — inherited from Kydland-Prescott, never defended
- Rogoff's third-best treated as first-best; free lunch claim (Alesina-Summers) drops his acknowledged trade-off
- Rogoff's own first-best (remove labour market distortions) encodes contestable neoclassical assumptions about labour markets
- De jure measurement conflates mandate content with operational autonomy
- Powell example: congressional mandate revision toward employment would *lower* Romelli independence score — proves circularity; conflates what mandate contains with how independently it is pursued
- US/Turkey anomaly: US scores below Turkey on de jure indices — yet Turkey is Posen's own paradigm case of lost independence causing inflation; qualitative and quantitative evidence point in opposite directions
- Two non-interacting epistemic communities demonstrated textually: Romelli does not cite Dal Bó; PIIE contributors do not engage capture literature
- Economics community institutionally embedded; political science critique has no institutional footprint — this is precisely what Zingales predicts
- Conclusion: assumptions systematically favour creditor interests over democratic ones

### 4.2 Vessels of Transmission (~1,800w)
*The assumptions travel from academic literature into institutional discourse through specific channels*

#### 4.2.1 Governors' speeches (~900w)
- Assumptions reproduced in institutional communication by governors with financial sector backgrounds
- Bernanke (*The Courage to Act*, 2015): Congress did not understand QE while it was happening — Dal Bó's asymmetric information mechanism confirmed by the Fed's own chair; epistemic dominance enables discretion
- PIIE 2025 as exhibit: epistemic community defending CBI while acknowledging capture risk only in supervisory functions — monetary policy mandate treated as beyond democratic contestation
- Adolph (2018): "central bankers with greater legal independence are more powerful agents... monetary policies selected by independent central banks are more subject to influences on monetary agents' preferences — especially influence from shadow principals"

#### 4.2.2 Revolving door (~900w)
- Personnel as transmission mechanism — epistemic community reproduces itself through appointments
- ABA revolving door: Olson (ABA president 1986–87 → Fed governor 2001–06); Duke (ABA chair 2004–05 → Fed governor 2007–13)
- Bowman: community bank background → Kansas banking commissioner → Trump nomination → Vice Chair for Supervision; SVB framed as management failure; supervision staff cut 30%; post-SVB reforms reversed
- Wirsching (2018): finance-sector backgrounds empirically predict deregulatory outcomes
- Adolph (2018): shadow principals — greater legal independence amplifies mechanism; career incentives shape preferences before and after office

### 4.3 Legislative Blockage (~2,450w)
*The defences of legislative inaction are rooted in the same assumptions exposed in 4.1 — the circularity is the argument*

- Volcker 1979: paradigm case — regime shift toward price stability with no legal change; dual mandate operationally overwritten without democratic revision; structural capture precedes rather than follows CBI entrenchment
- Lindblom (1982): market as prison — mandate revision triggers market volatility without any lobbying required; structural deterrent
- Dodd-Frank as failed critical juncture: reform channelled into adjustments (Class A director reform, stress testing) that foreclosed fundamental revision; post-2008 QE recapitalised financial sector, increasing political power precisely when reform pressure peaked (Jacobs and King 2018)
- ABA lobbying targets Fed charter revision directly — pre-emptive capture of reform agenda
- McCubbins and Schwartz (1984): fire alarm oversight — financial sector ensures alarms not pulled on mandate questions
- Bernanke/asymmetric information: legislative oversight structurally disabled by informational asymmetry — Congress cannot revise what it cannot understand
- PIIE 2025 as epistemic closure exhibit: the expert community advising Congress on monetary policy shares the institutional assumptions of the financial sector; defences of independence reproduce the assumptions of 4.1
- Rival hypothesis engagement: technocratic legitimacy explains outcome but not assumptions (4.1), personnel patterns (4.2), or redirected reform energy (4.3); rival hypothesis is itself a product of the captured epistemic environment; requires three independent coincidences where structural capture explains all with one mechanism

---

## 5. Conclusion (~650w)

- Epistemic capture as unifying mechanism: assumptions produced in captured environment → transmitted through personnel and institutional discourse → legislative revision made unthinkable
- Contribution 1: extends Dal Bó capture typology to monetary policy institutions
- Contribution 2: CBI measurement encodes preferences of a captured epistemic community — the empirical consensus rests on circular foundations; the literature legitimating CBI cannot be trusted to evaluate CBI neutrally
- Real-world implication: the policy consensus is circular — built on literature shaped by the interests it purports to study
- Caveat: this argument is categorically distinct from authoritarian executive interference with central bank personnel (Trump/Powell); that is a separate research agenda concerning authoritarianism and central banking
- Closing: the question is not whether the Fed should be independent from political interference — it is whether the institutional design serves democratic or financial sector interests, and whether the literature legitimating it can answer that question honestly
